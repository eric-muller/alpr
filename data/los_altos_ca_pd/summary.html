Organization audit: number of agencies and cameras

The first column is the number of agencies that queried the data, which by construction must be 1 in an organization audit.

The second column is the maximum number of cameras involved in a query. While most agencies operate a relatively small number of cameras, they can search data captured by other agencies.

The next column, “% 0-100” is the percentage of queries that involved less than 100 cameras. Similarly, the following columns are the percentages for 100 to 1k cameras, 1k to 10k cameras and more than 10k cameras respectively.

In the August-November 2024 period, there are few queries with a large number of cameras, so the maximum number of cameras is subject to wide variations. The low number in January 2025 was caused by a systemic problem with the Flock system. It remains that over the long term, more and more cameras are accessible.

If we ignore those months, it is clear that there is a bimodal distribution of the number of cameras: either a few cameras are searched (less than 100), or there is a broad search, with more than 1,000 cameras.

Also apparent is the increase in the proportion of broad queries over the last six months, roughly from 30% to 60%.

Network audit: number of cameras, in detail

This graph displays one dot for each query, with the time of the query as the x coordinate and the number of cameras as the y coordinate. A few agencies get their own color. (Click on the image for a larger view)

The two groups at the top correspond to the period when participated in National Lookup: many of the queries in those two groups were made by thousands of agencies outside California. That period started on 2024-12-05 and ended on 2025-03-19.

The right group can be also be split in two: from 2025-02-13 on, all the queries came from a dozen of California agencies.

A plausible interpretation is that turned National Lookup off around 2025-02-13. After that point, non California agencies no longer had access to data, so they no longer appear in the logs (not that they stopped making broad queries). All the queries were from Calfornia agencies that were still participating in National Lookup. Then on 2025-03-19, National Lookup was finally turned off by Flock for all California agencies.

 

In the gap between the two blocks, most (but not all) queries are recorded as accessing 0 cameras. The cause is unknown.

 

El Cajon is notorious for its February 11, 2025 resolution 015-25 (page 87), and as of November 27, 2025 is overtly violating SB40 by sharing its ALPR data with many agencies outside California.

 

Note that queries with a large number of cameras are not necessarily an indication of improper sharing. Vacaville, for example, is authorized by many agencies outside of California, but does not reciprocate. The same is even more true for the Riverside County DA, which probably does not control any cameras.

Conversely, queries with a small number of cameras are not necessarily an indication that there is no improper sharing. An agency could elect to systematically search fewer cameras than it has access to.
